Growing Labor Market Precarity

An exploration of the uneven development of contingent labor across the United States.

About the Project

Contingent labor, flexible labor, non-standard labor arrangements, temporary labor, on-demand labor, or any of the vast array of classifications used to describe the labor relations of workers who lack a permanent connection to traditional employment, has been a rising portion of the labor market, since the 1970’s (Gleason, 2006; Liu and Kolenda, 2012). The contingent labor market spans from low-skill labor such as day laborers and trade workers, to more specialized workers within the white-collar or tech industries (Peck and Theodore, 2001; Scholz, 2017). In the late 1990’s the contingent labor market expanded, particularly into geographies with concentrations of low-skill labor and a lack of job opportunities (Peck and Theodore, 2001). More recently, the expansion of on-demand labor has been propelled by technological advances which allow workers to utilize smart phones, apps and other mobile or internet based platforms to access employment (Scholz, 2017). Labor markets are changing, and while the definition of contingent labor varies making it difficult to determine the proportion of workers who have job insecurity or precarious employment arrangements, estimates range from 5%, to over a third of the labor market (Liu & Kolenda, 2012; Accountability Report, 2015). Important questions arise from the recent increases in contingent or non-standard labor: Under what conditions does contingent or non-standard labor increase? What role do cities and the state play in this rise? And what is the geographic relationship of the rise in contingent labor?
